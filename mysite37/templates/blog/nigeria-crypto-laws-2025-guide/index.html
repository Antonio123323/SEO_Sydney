The History of Cryptocurrency in Africa

Cryptocurrency’s presence in Africa started quietly over a decade ago. In the early 2010s, only a handful of tech enthusiasts knew about Bitcoin. Most people relied on cash or mobile money services, such as Kenya’s M-Pesa, because banks were often unavailable or too expensive, especially in rural regions.

Things began to shift as smartphones and mobile internet became more common. This made it easier for people to access digital wallets and crypto apps. The global surge in Bitcoin’s value around 2017 brought new attention to digital currencies.

Many Africans found cryptocurrency useful for several reasons:

  • Inflation protection: In countries like Nigeria and Zimbabwe, local currencies lost value quickly, so people used crypto to preserve their wealth.
  • Affordable remittances: Traditional money transfers across borders were costly. Crypto provided a faster and cheaper alternative.
  • Financial inclusion: Millions without bank accounts could still access crypto via their phones, opening new doors to the financial system.

By 2021, Africa had some of the fastest-growing crypto communities worldwide. Peer-to-peer (P2P) platforms became a popular way to trade crypto without involving banks.

Governments reacted differently — some embraced innovation, others expressed concern over risks like money laundering or loss of control over national currencies. This led to varied approaches across the continent, from supportive regulation to outright bans.

Nigeria emerged as a leading market with a vibrant crypto scene but also strict government policies. In the next section, we explore Nigeria’s unique journey from crypto enthusiasm to formal regulation.

Case Study: Nigeria

Nigeria stands out as Africa’s largest crypto market, where millions trade and invest in Bitcoin, stablecoins, and other digital currencies. For many Nigerians, crypto is a tool to safeguard their wealth from inflation or to transfer money internationally.

However, Nigeria’s stance on cryptocurrency has evolved significantly over the years. Below is a summary of key developments shaping the country’s crypto landscape.

Key Events in Nigeria’s Crypto Regulation

The 2021 Bank Ban and Its Effects

In early 2021, the Central Bank of Nigeria ordered all banks to stop providing services to cryptocurrency businesses. This disrupted many exchanges but pushed users toward peer-to-peer trading channels. As a result, Nigeria saw a surge in P2P Bitcoin volumes on platforms like Paxful and Binance.

ISA 2026: Formal Recognition of Crypto

In April 2025, Nigeria passed the Investment and Securities Act, which classifies cryptocurrencies as digital securities regulated by the SEC. This new legal framework aims to protect investors and bring clarity to the market.

Challenges and Legal Battles

Despite progress, tensions remain. The Central Bank maintains a cautious stance, and banks are still reluctant to engage with crypto-related transactions. Legal disputes, including a major case against Binance for alleged tax evasion and money laundering, highlight ongoing friction.

South Africa and Countries with Moderate Regulation

Not every African country has taken a strict stance on cryptocurrency. Some nations have chosen a balanced approach, allowing crypto activity under clear rules to protect users while encouraging innovation.

South Africa: A Pioneer in Crypto Rules

South Africa has been at the forefront of crypto regulation in Africa. Its Financial Sector Conduct Authority (FSCA) started crafting guidelines early on. By 2022, the FSCA declared cryptocurrencies as “financial products,” requiring providers to register and comply with specific regulations.

While crypto isn’t legal tender—meaning it can’t be used to pay for everyday goods—it is fully legal to buy, hold, and trade. Users must also report profits from crypto trading for tax purposes.

Mauritius: A Growing Fintech Hub

Mauritius has embraced digital assets as part of its fintech strategy. Since 2018, it has regulated crypto through licenses granted by the Financial Services Commission (FSC). This openness attracts startups and international blockchain businesses.

Other Countries Taking Moderate Steps

  • Uganda: Promotes blockchain for government services but lacks specific crypto laws.
  • Ghana: Issues warnings about crypto risks but does not ban trading.
  • Botswana: Provides licensing for crypto exchanges but has low overall crypto adoption.

This mix of regulation and caution shows that Africa’s crypto future is diverse. While some countries impose bans, others recognize the potential and seek to control risks through smart rules.

Countries with Partial Bans and Banking Restrictions

Several African nations haven't outright banned cryptocurrency but have imposed strict banking restrictions that make it difficult to use. These measures are often called "banking bans." Essentially, crypto activities remain legal, but banks are not allowed to provide services to crypto businesses or users.

How These Restrictions Work

Central banks in these countries instruct commercial banks to:

  • Close accounts linked to crypto exchanges
  • Block transactions to or from crypto wallets
  • Prevent the use of bank cards for crypto purchases

This forces many users to rely on peer-to-peer trading methods, which can be risky due to lack of oversight and higher chances of fraud.

Examples of Affected Countries

Kenya: Since 2015, the Central Bank has discouraged banks from working with crypto firms. Despite this, many Kenyans actively trade Bitcoin and stablecoins via unofficial channels.

Tanzania: The government has warned against crypto use and restricted banking services for crypto businesses. Although discussions about regulatory changes continue, formal banking access remains limited.

Cameroon and Democratic Republic of Congo: Both countries lack formal crypto laws but maintain banking restrictions that hinder crypto transactions.

Impact on Users

These banking bans push crypto trading underground. Many users turn to WhatsApp, Telegram, and informal networks to buy and sell digital assets. This creates a high-risk environment where scams are common.

Full Bans and Criminal Penalties

Some African countries have taken a strict approach and fully banned the use of cryptocurrencies. In these places, owning, trading, or using crypto is illegal and can lead to fines or even jail time.

Countries with Complete Crypto Prohibitions

  • Algeria: Since 2018, Algeria has banned all cryptocurrency activities. Those caught can face serious penalties.
  • Morocco: Cryptocurrency trading is illegal. The government blocks related websites and payment methods.
  • Egypt: The government considers crypto harmful to the economy. Religious authorities also declared it forbidden under Islamic law.
  • Zimbabwe: Banks are prohibited from supporting crypto. Despite this, underground trading continues.

Reasons for Bans

Governments often cite concerns such as:

  • Loss of control over capital and national currency
  • Risks of fraud, scams, and money laundering
  • Difficulty in regulating and taxing crypto activities
  • Religious objections, as in some Islamic countries

Continued Underground Use

Despite bans, many individuals find ways to trade cryptocurrencies secretly using VPNs and foreign platforms. However, this involves significant risks.

Economic and Political Reasons Behind Regulation

Crypto laws in Africa aren’t made randomly. They reflect deeper economic and political issues. Here are the main reasons why governments regulate or ban cryptocurrencies.

Protecting National Currency

Many African currencies face inflation and instability. Crypto allows people to move money outside the official system, which can weaken the local currency. Governments want to keep control over their money supply.

Tax Collection and Capital Control

Cryptocurrency transactions can be hard to track. This makes it difficult for governments to collect taxes or control the flow of money across borders. Strict regulations help prevent illegal money movements.

Influence of Global Organizations

Groups like the International Monetary Fund (IMF) and the Financial Action Task Force (FATF) push countries to adopt anti-money laundering rules. These pressures often lead to tighter crypto laws.

Central Bank Digital Currencies (CBDCs)

Some African countries are creating their own digital currencies, called CBDCs. These government-backed currencies aim to offer the benefits of crypto without the risks. Nigeria’s eNaira is an example.

Political Stability

Cryptocurrency can empower people financially. In some countries, governments fear it could also fund political opposition or protests. Controlling crypto is seen as a way to maintain stability.

These factors explain why crypto regulation in Africa is about more than technology — it’s about power, control, and economics.

Trends and Where Africa Is Heading

Africa’s crypto scene is growing fast, despite challenges. Here are some key trends shaping its future.

More People Using Crypto

Younger Africans are driving adoption. Many use crypto to save money, send payments, or start businesses. Mobile internet and smartphone use keep expanding, making crypto more accessible.

Rise of Local Crypto Platforms

Africa is seeing more homegrown exchanges and wallets. These services cater to local languages and currencies. Examples include Yellow Card in Nigeria, VALR in South Africa, and BitSika in Ghana.

Governments Moving Toward Regulation

Instead of banning crypto, many countries are working on clear rules and licensing systems. South Africa, Mauritius, and Nigeria are leaders in this trend.

Risks and Challenges

While cryptocurrency offers many opportunities, it also brings risks, especially in Africa where regulation and education are still developing. Here are the main challenges users face:

Scams and Fraud

Fake schemes and Ponzi scams are widespread. Many people lose money because they don’t recognize fraudulent platforms or offers.

Lack of User Protection

In many countries, there are no laws to protect crypto users. If funds are stolen or platforms fail, users have little legal recourse.

Confusing Regulations

Mixed signals from governments and regulators create uncertainty. Some agencies may support crypto, while others restrict it, confusing both users and businesses.

Limited Access

Issues like unreliable internet, electricity, and banking infrastructure make crypto less accessible, especially in rural areas.

Price Volatility

Cryptocurrency prices can change rapidly. This can lead to unexpected losses for inexperienced investors.
