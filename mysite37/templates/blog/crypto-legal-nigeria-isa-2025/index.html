Crypto Is Now Legal in Nigeria — But There’s a Catch

  • Jean Dupont
  • 12/10/2025
Home Blog Etherealux Flux

Introduction

Cryptocurrency is growing fast in Nigeria. From students trading Bitcoin on their phones to small businesses using stablecoins for cross-border payments — crypto is becoming part of everyday life. Nigeria now ranks among the top countries in peer-to-peer (P2P) crypto usage.

But until recently, the legal status of crypto in Nigeria was confusing. The Central Bank of Nigeria (CBN) had banned banks from dealing with crypto-related businesses since 201 This created fear, blocked exchanges, and pushed most activity into the shadows.

In 2026, things changed. The Nigerian government passed a new law — the Investment and Securities Act (ISA) 2026. For the first time, Nigeria officially recognized digital assets such as cryptocurrencies, utility tokens, stablecoins, and NFTs.

Now, there are clear rules. The Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) is in charge of regulating crypto activities. There are new requirements for companies, exchanges, and investors.

So, is cryptocurrency legal in Nigeria? Yes — but it’s regulated. In this article, we explain what the new crypto laws mean for individuals, businesses, and investors. You’ll learn what’s allowed, who’s in control, and what steps to take if you’re involved in crypto in Nigeria.

Brief History of Crypto Regulation in Nigeria Before 2026

Before 2026, crypto regulation in Nigeria was unclear and unstable. Most people used digital assets without legal protection or official guidance. This made the market risky and hard to trust.

2017–2021: CBN Restrictions and Banking Bans

In 2017, the Central Bank of Nigeria (CBN) issued its first warning about cryptocurrencies. It told banks not to support crypto transactions, calling them risky and unregulated. In February 2021, CBN went further. It banned all banks and financial institutions from dealing with crypto-related accounts. Many platforms had to shut down or move to peer-to-peer (P2P) models.

As a result, investors started trading directly with each other using P2P platforms. Exchanges like Binance and Paxful became popular for Nigerian users. But there was still no law that made crypto fully legal or illegal. People operated in a legal "grey area."

Attempts at Regulation

At the same time, the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) tried to bring some order. In 2020, SEC released a statement saying that digital assets would be treated as securities. But with CBN bans still in place, this guidance was hard to enforce. The two institutions had different approaches.

The Rise of eNaira

In 2021, Nigeria launched its own Central Bank Digital Currency (CBDC), called the eNaira. The goal was to offer a safer, regulated alternative to cryptocurrencies. But adoption remained low. Many Nigerians still preferred crypto for its flexibility, access to global markets, and resistance to inflation.

This mix of bans, warnings, and partial support created confusion. Investors had no solid legal ground. Companies couldn’t get licenses. Many people feared using crypto openly.

All of this changed with the new ISA 2026. For the first time, crypto became part of official Nigerian law.

What Is ISA 2026 and Why Does It Matter?

The Investment and Securities Act (ISA) 2026 is a new law passed by the Nigerian government in March 202 It replaces the older ISA 2007 and brings major updates to how investments are handled in Nigeria — including cryptocurrency.

For the first time, the Nigerian government officially recognizes digital assets such as cryptocurrencies, tokens, and NFTs as part of the legal investment system. This is a big step forward from years of bans and silence.

Who Passed the Law?

The ISA 2026 was signed into law by President Bola Ahmed Tinubu after being approved by the National Assembly. It was supported by several ministries and the Nigerian SEC (Securities and Exchange Commission), showing strong government commitment.

Why Is ISA 2026 Important?

  • Legal Recognition: The law clearly defines crypto as "securities" under Nigerian regulation. This removes uncertainty for users and businesses.
  • Investor Protection: The SEC can now regulate crypto companies, protect users, and stop scams and illegal activities.
  • Market Confidence: Local and global investors now have a legal framework to operate in Nigeria’s digital asset space.

Main Goals of the Law

ISA 2026 focuses on four key objectives:

  • Transparency – Making the crypto market more open and secure.
  • Regulation – Giving the SEC tools to monitor and control crypto activities.
  • Inclusion – Allowing more people access to regulated investment products.
  • Innovation – Encouraging fintech growth and safer digital finance solutions.

In simple terms, ISA 2026 gives crypto a legal home in Nigeria. It sets the stage for new opportunities, safer investments, and long-term trust in the market.

Key Provisions of ISA 2026 on Crypto and Digital Assets

ISA 2026 introduces clear definitions and rules for how cryptocurrencies and other digital assets are treated under Nigerian law. This is one of the most important parts of the law for anyone involved in crypto — whether you're an investor, developer, or exchange operator.

What Is Now Recognized?

The law officially recognizes several types of digital assets, including:

  • Cryptocurrencies like Bitcoin and Ethereum
  • Stablecoins backed by fiat currencies
  • Utility tokens used in decentralized platforms
  • NFTs (Non-Fungible Tokens) for digital ownership

These assets are now classified as "securities" under Section 357 of ISA 202 This means they are subject to the same rules as traditional investment products like stocks and bonds.

Key Legal Terms

ISA 2026 uses specific terms to describe the crypto space:

  • Digital Asset: Any asset stored or transferred using blockchain technology
  • Virtual Asset: A digital representation of value that can be traded digitally
  • Token: A digital unit that can represent ownership, utility, or rights

These definitions are now part of Nigerian law. They create a legal structure that helps courts, regulators, and businesses apply consistent rules.

Investor Rights and Protections

By treating crypto as securities, ISA 2026 gives investors stronger protection. Platforms must now follow rules on:

  • Transparency and regular reporting
  • Disclosure of project risks and structures
  • Anti-fraud monitoring
  • Protection of user funds

The SEC also has the power to freeze suspicious accounts, shut down illegal platforms, and block scams. This helps build trust in the digital asset space.

Why It Matters

Before ISA 2026, Nigerian crypto users had no legal protections. Now, there are rules that can protect buyers from fraud and hold platforms accountable. It also means crypto businesses must operate transparently — or face penalties.

For investors, this makes Nigeria a safer place to buy, hold, and trade digital assets.

The Regulator: Who Now Controls the Market?

With the new law in place, the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) is officially in charge of regulating Nigeria’s crypto market. This is a major shift from the past, where both SEC and the Central Bank of Nigeria (CBN) gave conflicting signals.

What Powers Does the SEC Have?

ISA 2026 gives the SEC wide control over digital asset activities. Here’s what the SEC can now do:

  • Register crypto companies like exchanges and wallet providers
  • Monitor market activity and enforce rules
  • Investigate fraud and freeze suspicious accounts
  • Approve or reject digital tokens offered to the public

The SEC can also work with other regulators, such as the CBN, the Nigerian Communications Commission (NCC), and law enforcement to track illegal behavior and protect investors.

How This Affects Investors

For everyday crypto users, this means:

  • You are now protected by a legal authority if something goes wrong
  • You can verify if a crypto business is licensed and approved
  • You have access to regulated services that follow safety standards

It also means crypto companies must be more careful and transparent. They have to follow KYC (Know Your Customer) and AML (Anti-Money Laundering) rules, file reports, and keep user funds safe.

Possible Challenges

Although the SEC is now the main regulator, some confusion remains. The Central Bank still controls how naira is used in the banking system, and it has not fully opened the doors for crypto yet. This overlapping control may slow down progress in some areas.

Still, having one clear regulator for crypto assets is a big win for investors. It brings more order, accountability, and safety to the Nigerian crypto market.

New Requirements for Crypto Companies and Platforms (VASPs)

Under ISA 2026, any company dealing with digital assets in Nigeria must register as a Virtual Asset Service Provider (VASP). This includes crypto exchanges, token issuers, wallet providers, and even foreign platforms offering services to Nigerians.

Who Needs to Register?

The following types of companies must register with the Nigerian SEC:

  • Centralized crypto exchanges (like Binance or Luno)
  • Wallet providers and custodial platforms
  • Token issuers and developers launching new coins
  • Crypto investment platforms and asset managers

Without SEC approval, these companies are not allowed to operate legally in Nigeria.

Capital and Insurance Requirements

ISA 2026 sets financial rules to ensure companies are stable and trustworthy:

  • Minimum capital: ₦500 million (Naira) for VASPs
  • Fidelity bond: Insurance covering at least 25% of capital

These measures are meant to protect users if a company fails or funds go missing.

AML and KYC Obligations

VASPs must follow strict anti-money laundering (AML) and know-your-customer (KYC) policies:

  • Verify identities of users
  • Report suspicious activity to the authorities
  • Keep transaction records

Companies must also publish clear terms, disclose risks, and provide a secure trading environment.

Exchange Categories

ISA 2026 creates two types of exchanges:

  • Composite Exchanges: Offer both crypto and traditional securities
  • Non-Composite Exchanges: Deal only with digital assets

Each must follow different rules depending on its structure and services.

Penalties for Non-Compliance

Companies that ignore these rules can face heavy penalties, including:

  • Fines and seizure of assets
  • Revocation of operating licenses
  • Public blacklisting by the SEC

Foreign companies that operate without approval can also be blocked or restricted.

These new requirements raise the bar for crypto businesses in Nigeria. But they also create a safer and more trustworthy market for investors.

Remaining Risks and Limitations Despite the Law

Even though ISA 2026 brings clarity and structure, not all problems are solved. There are still some challenges and restrictions that affect how crypto works in Nigeria.

CBN Restrictions Still in Place

The Central Bank of Nigeria (CBN) has not fully accepted crypto. Banks are still restricted from directly supporting crypto transactions. This means:

  • Users cannot easily deposit or withdraw naira to crypto exchanges using regular bank accounts
  • Some exchanges use workarounds like P2P or third-party payment services

This policy creates a gap between the goals of the SEC and the current position of the CBN. While the law recognizes crypto, the banking system remains partly closed to it.

Exchange Blocks and Platform Shutdowns

In early 2024 and 2026, the Nigerian government blocked access to several crypto platforms, including Binance and Bybit. The reason was related to concerns about currency manipulation and money laundering.

These actions show that the government can still restrict platforms — even if crypto is now legal under ISA 202 This creates uncertainty for users and businesses.

Limits on P2P Trading

Peer-to-peer (P2P) trading has been a key part of Nigeria’s crypto ecosystem. But due to misuse, especially involving the naira, regulators are cracking down. Some users report delayed transactions, frozen accounts, or blocked wallet apps.

This affects the most popular and accessible way people trade crypto in Nigeria.

Dual Regulation Risks

Because both the SEC and CBN are involved in crypto-related rules, there is still risk of conflicting policies. For example, SEC may license a company, while CBN may block their banking access.

Until both institutions fully align, the Nigerian crypto space will face mixed signals.

Impact on Investors

Retail users may still feel cautious. Institutional investors may hesitate due to legal uncertainty. Even with ISA 2026 in place, real-world application depends on cooperation between all regulators and the government’s future actions.

ISA 2026 is a major milestone, but Nigeria still has work to do to become a fully open and crypto-friendly environment.

What This Means for Investors: Opportunities and Challenges

With ISA 2026 in effect, the rules for crypto investing in Nigeria are clearer than ever before. But the new law brings both opportunities and challenges for investors of all sizes.

New Opportunities

  • Legal Protection: Investors now have official protection under Nigerian law. If something goes wrong, they can report issues to the SEC.
  • Trusted Platforms: Only licensed and registered crypto companies can operate legally. This increases market safety and reduces scams.
  • Global Confidence: With a real regulatory framework, foreign investors and companies may be more willing to enter the Nigerian market.
  • More Products: Tokenized assets, stablecoins, and even traditional investments may become available through secure digital platforms.

Challenges to Consider

  • Higher Compliance Costs: Companies must meet strict capital and insurance rules. These costs may be passed on to users through higher fees.
  • P2P Uncertainty: P2P platforms remain popular but face pressure from the government. Users may see delays, restrictions, or platform shutdowns.
  • Regulatory Overlap: The ongoing tension between SEC and CBN could create confusion, especially for newer investors.

Investors need to be aware of these changes and adjust their strategies. Choosing only licensed platforms, verifying company status with the SEC, and keeping up with law updates will be key to staying protected.

More importantly, ISA 2026 marks the transition from a grey market to a regulated crypto economy. This is a big step toward building a digital financial future in Nigeria.

The Future of Cryptocurrency in Nigeria: Post-ISA 2026 Outlook

ISA 2026 is not just about rules — it’s a signal that Nigeria is opening its doors to a digital economy. The future of cryptocurrency in the country looks more promising, but also depends on how well the new law is implemented.

Integration with Global Markets

With clear crypto laws, Nigeria is better positioned to work with global crypto companies and attract international investment. Legal recognition may also encourage more exchanges and fintech firms to enter the Nigerian market — if they meet SEC requirements.

Regulatory Alignment

One of the biggest factors for future growth will be cooperation between the SEC and CBN. If both institutions align their policies, it could unlock full access to crypto services through the banking system. This would reduce reliance on P2P and improve trust in the ecosystem.

Startup and Innovation Boom

Nigerian developers and entrepreneurs can now build within a legal framework. Tokenized real estate, NFT marketplaces, DeFi apps — all can now operate more safely. The country may see a surge in crypto innovation, especially among its tech-savvy youth.

More Institutional Participation

Banks, asset managers, and traditional finance players may begin exploring regulated crypto products. This could lead to ETFs, regulated crypto savings accounts, or even pension fund exposure — once policies mature.

Overall, ISA 2026 lays a strong foundation. With continued development, Nigeria could become one of Africa’s leading crypto economies.

Conclusion

So, is cryptocurrency legal in Nigeria? Yes — under the Investment and Securities Act (ISA) 2026, digital assets like Bitcoin, stablecoins, NFTs, and tokens are now officially recognized and regulated. This marks a major turning point in Nigeria’s approach to crypto.

The SEC has been given clear authority to license, supervise, and protect the market. Crypto companies must meet new standards, and investors now have legal rights and recourse. These changes build trust, reduce fraud, and open the door to long-term growth.

However, some risks remain. Restrictions from the Central Bank, P2P limitations, and the need for clearer coordination across agencies could slow down full adoption. But the direction is clear: Nigeria is moving toward a regulated and inclusive crypto future.
