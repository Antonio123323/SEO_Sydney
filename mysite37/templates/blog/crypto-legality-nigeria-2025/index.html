The History of Cryptocurrency in Africa

Cryptocurrency’s journey in Africa began quietly more than a decade ago. Back in the early 2010s, Bitcoin and other digital coins were known mainly among tech enthusiasts. Most people still relied on cash or popular mobile money services like Kenya’s M-Pesa because banks were often inaccessible or expensive, especially in rural areas.

Over time, as smartphones and internet access expanded, more Africans started exploring digital currencies. The Bitcoin price surge in 2017 brought greater attention, sparking wider interest across the continent.

Africans turned to crypto for several key reasons:

  • Protecting savings: Countries like Nigeria and Zimbabwe face high inflation, causing local currencies to lose value fast. Crypto offered a way to hold wealth more securely.
  • Affordable cross-border payments: Sending money abroad through traditional channels can be expensive and slow. Crypto offered a cheaper, faster alternative.
  • Financial inclusion: Millions without bank accounts could still use crypto via mobile phones, opening up new financial possibilities.

By 2021, Africa had become one of the fastest-growing regions for peer-to-peer (P2P) crypto trading. This growth caught the attention of governments—some welcomed the innovation, others worried about risks like money laundering or loss of monetary control. Different countries responded with varying policies, ranging from supportive regulation to outright bans.

Nigeria emerged as a key player with one of the largest crypto markets but also complex regulatory shifts. The next section dives deeper into Nigeria’s crypto story.

Case Study: Nigeria

Nigeria is Africa’s leading crypto market, with millions of people buying, selling, and investing in digital currencies like Bitcoin and stablecoins. Many Nigerians use crypto to protect their savings from inflation or to send money abroad.

However, Nigeria’s approach to cryptocurrency has evolved over the years. Here are the main milestones:

Key Moments in Nigeria’s Crypto Regulation

Year Event Impact
2017 Central Bank issues first warning on crypto risks Public advised but no ban enforced
2021 Central Bank bans banks from servicing crypto firms Growth of peer-to-peer trading
2022 Launch of eNaira, Nigeria’s digital currency Limited adoption by the public
2026 Investment and Securities Act (ISA) officially regulates crypto SEC takes charge of crypto regulation

The 2021 Banking Ban

In 2021, Nigeria’s Central Bank ordered banks to stop providing services to crypto companies. This forced many users and exchanges to switch to peer-to-peer platforms. As a result, Nigeria became a global leader in P2P Bitcoin trading volumes.

ISA 2026: Legal Recognition and Regulation

In 2026, Nigeria passed the Investment and Securities Act, recognizing cryptocurrencies as digital securities under the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC). This law aims to protect investors and bring clarity to the market.

Ongoing Challenges

Despite the new law, tensions remain. Banks are still cautious about dealing with crypto, and legal battles continue, including actions against major exchanges for alleged violations.

South Africa and Countries with Moderate Regulation

Not all African countries have taken a strict approach to cryptocurrency. Some have found a middle ground, allowing crypto activity with clear regulations to protect users while encouraging innovation.

South Africa: Leading with Clear Rules

South Africa has been a pioneer in regulating crypto on the continent. The Financial Sector Conduct Authority (FSCA) has established guidelines declaring cryptocurrencies as “financial products.” Providers must register and comply with regulations.

Crypto is not legal tender—meaning it can’t be used for everyday purchases—but buying, holding, and trading digital currencies is allowed. Crypto gains are also taxable.

Mauritius: A Growing Fintech Hub

Mauritius supports digital assets through licensing by its Financial Services Commission (FSC). It’s becoming a regional center for blockchain startups and international crypto businesses.

Other Moderate Countries

  • Uganda: Promotes blockchain but lacks specific crypto laws.
  • Ghana: Warns about risks but does not ban crypto.
  • Botswana: Issues licenses for crypto exchanges but has low adoption.

This range of policies shows Africa’s diverse approach to cryptocurrency regulation. Some countries ban crypto, while others aim to balance safety and growth.

Countries with Partial Bans and Banking Restrictions

In several African countries, cryptocurrencies aren’t fully banned, but banks are restricted from supporting crypto businesses. This approach is often called a banking ban. It means crypto use is legal, but banking services for crypto-related activities are limited or blocked.

How Banking Restrictions Work

Central banks direct financial institutions to:

  • Close accounts connected to crypto platforms
  • Block transactions involving crypto wallets
  • Prevent use of bank cards for crypto purchases

This pushes crypto trading into peer-to-peer (P2P) networks, which can be riskier due to less oversight.

Countries with Banking Restrictions

Kenya: The Central Bank has discouraged banks from engaging with crypto firms since 2015, yet P2P trading remains strong.

Tanzania: Banks are prohibited from servicing crypto companies, and the government continues to review its stance.

Cameroon and the Democratic Republic of Congo: These countries have no formal crypto laws but maintain banking restrictions to limit crypto use.

Impact on Users

Banking bans force many users to trade crypto informally via social media and messaging apps. This increases the risk of scams and fraud.

Full Bans and Criminal Penalties

Some African countries have taken a hard stance by completely banning cryptocurrencies. In these nations, owning, trading, or using digital currencies is illegal and can lead to fines or imprisonment.

Countries with Complete Crypto Bans

  • Algeria: Since 2018, all cryptocurrency activities are prohibited, with penalties for offenders.
  • Morocco: Crypto trading is illegal, and authorities block related websites and payment channels.
  • Egypt: Crypto is considered harmful economically, and religious leaders have declared it forbidden under Islamic law.
  • Zimbabwe: Banks cannot service crypto businesses, though underground trading continues.

Reasons Behind the Bans

Governments cite concerns like:

  • Loss of control over currency and capital
  • Potential for fraud and money laundering
  • Lack of effective regulation and taxation
  • Religious objections in some countries

Underground Crypto Use

Despite bans, many still trade crypto secretly using VPNs and foreign platforms, facing high risks.

Economic and Political Reasons Behind Regulation

Crypto regulations in Africa reflect broader economic and political concerns. Here are the main factors influencing governments’ decisions.

Protecting Local Currencies

Many African currencies suffer from inflation and instability. Crypto use can weaken local currencies as people move money outside official channels. Governments want to maintain control over their economies.

Taxation and Capital Flow Control

Cryptocurrency transactions are harder to track, making tax collection and capital control difficult. Regulations help governments monitor and control money movements.

Global Influence

International bodies like the IMF and FATF push for anti-money laundering rules, encouraging stricter crypto laws.

Central Bank Digital Currencies (CBDCs)

Countries like Nigeria have launched CBDCs, offering government-backed digital money as a safer alternative to crypto.

Political Stability

Unregulated crypto can empower citizens, which some governments see as a threat to political control.

In summary, crypto laws are about control and stability as much as technology.
