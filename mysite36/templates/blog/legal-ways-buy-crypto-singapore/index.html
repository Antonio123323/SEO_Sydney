Regulations and Crypto Laws in Singapore

Singapore takes a strong and clear approach to crypto regulation. It doesn’t ban cryptocurrencies — instead, it controls how companies offer these services to protect users and prevent misuse. If you plan to buy or trade crypto in Singapore, understanding the rules will help you avoid mistakes.

The country’s main financial authority is the Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS). It supervises digital asset providers through a licensing system. Any exchange or crypto platform operating in Singapore must apply for a Digital Payment Token (DPT) license. Only licensed platforms are allowed to offer crypto services to people living in Singapore.

New Rules Starting in 2026

MAS has introduced new updates that will fully take effect on June 30, 2025. These changes aim to improve user safety and limit risky behavior. Here’s what you should know:

  • Credit card purchases will be restricted. Local cards cannot be used to buy crypto after the deadline.
  • Advertising rules are stricter. Crypto companies can’t make promises about high profits or guaranteed returns.
  • Customer funds must be kept separate. Platforms must store your money in independent accounts, not mix it with their own.

Failing to follow these rules can lead to serious penalties — including fines up to SGD 250,000 and even jail time. That’s why choosing a licensed and compliant platform is so important.

Some platforms let you buy crypto using a debit card or mobile payment tools like Apple Pay or Google Pay. This is a fast method, perfect for small or first-time purchases.

If you're investing a large amount — for example, more than SGD 10,000 — you may want to use an OTC service. These private services offer better rates and personalized help for high-volume traders.

After 2026, MAS rules will tighten further, and P2P platforms without a license may no longer operate in Singapore. For this reason, this method is not recommended unless you're highly experienced.

How to Store Your Cryptocurrency Safely

Once you’ve purchased cryptocurrency, the next important step is keeping it safe. While most exchanges offer built-in wallets, it’s usually not the best idea to leave your coins there for a long time. If something happens to the exchange — like a hack or technical failure — your assets could be lost.

To protect your investment, choose the right type of crypto wallet. There are two main options: hot wallets and cold wallets.

Hot Wallets

Hot wallets are connected to the internet. They include mobile apps, browser-based wallets, and exchange wallets. They are very convenient for quick access and daily transactions, but they are also more exposed to online threats.

  • Easy to use for beginners
  • Instant access for trading
  • Higher risk if not protected with strong passwords and 2FA

Tip: Platforms like Crypto.com and Gemini provide hot wallets with extra security options, like biometric login and insurance coverage.

Cold Wallets

Cold wallets are offline storage devices. They are the most secure option because they are not connected to the internet and cannot be easily hacked. These wallets are best for long-term holders who don’t need daily access.

  • Hardware wallets: Devices like Ledger Nano X or Trezor keep your crypto offline.
  • Paper wallets: A printed version of your wallet’s private key or QR code, stored in a safe place.

Important: Always back up your recovery phrase and store it somewhere safe. Losing it means losing access to your funds.

For serious investors, combining both types of wallets is a smart move — use a hot wallet for small amounts and a cold wallet for long-term savings. This way, you stay secure without giving up flexibility.

Crypto Taxes and Legal Aspects

Singapore is known for its friendly tax policies, and this also applies to cryptocurrency. Still, it's important to understand what is — and isn’t — taxed when you buy, sell, or hold digital assets in the country.

No Capital Gains Tax

In Singapore, there is no capital gains tax on personal investments. This means if you buy Bitcoin and later sell it for a profit, you won’t need to pay tax on that gain — as long as you're not running a business based on trading.

If you trade crypto professionally or earn from crypto-related services (like mining or staking as a business), your profits may be considered taxable income. In that case, you'll need to declare them to the Inland Revenue Authority of Singapore (IRAS).

Goods and Services Tax (GST)

Most major cryptocurrencies, such as Bitcoin and Ethereum, are considered Digital Payment Tokens (DPTs) by MAS. These tokens are exempt from GST, so you won’t be charged tax when you buy or sell them.

However, tokens that do not meet the DPT definition may still be subject to GST. This includes some utility tokens or digital vouchers. When in doubt, consult a tax advisor.

Record-Keeping and Reporting

While individuals don’t need to file reports just for holding crypto, it’s a good habit to keep a clear record of:

  • When and how much crypto you purchased
  • Transaction amounts and exchange rates
  • Wallet addresses and receipts

This information can help if you ever need to declare income or prove ownership for cross-border transfers.

Note: Crypto is legal to own and use in Singapore, but using it for illegal activity like fraud or money laundering is strictly punished under local law.

Staying informed about tax laws ensures your crypto journey stays legal and stress-free.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Even in a well-regulated country like Singapore, crypto users can run into trouble if they’re not careful. Here are some common mistakes and how to avoid them.

1. Using Unlicensed Platforms

Some websites and apps may look professional but don’t have approval from MAS. Using these platforms can put your money at serious risk. They may disappear without notice or fail to follow legal standards.

Tip: Always check the MAS website to confirm that a platform is officially licensed.

2. Paying with Credit Cards

Starting in 2026, crypto purchases with Singapore-issued credit cards will be restricted. Even today, using credit cards may lead to high fees or blocked transactions. Instead, use FAST or PayNow for smoother transfers.

3. Keeping Everything on the Exchange

After buying crypto, many users forget to move their assets to a safer place. Exchanges can be hacked or go offline. For better protection, move your crypto to a personal wallet — especially if you’re not trading daily.

4. Losing Access to Wallet Backups

If you lose your recovery phrase or private key, there is no way to recover your crypto. Store your backup in a secure offline location that only you can access.

Avoiding these mistakes will help you build a more secure and successful crypto journey from the start.
