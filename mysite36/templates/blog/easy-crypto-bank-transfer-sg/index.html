Legal Status of Crypto in Singapore

Singapore is one of Asia’s most crypto-friendly countries. You can buy, sell, and own digital assets like Bitcoin or Ethereum without breaking the law. However, it’s important to understand how crypto is treated by the government.

In Singapore, crypto is not considered legal money. You can’t use it to pay for goods like you use SGD. Instead, it is seen as a Digital Payment Token (DPT). This means you are free to use and trade crypto, but only under clear rules.

The main law that regulates crypto in Singapore is the Payment Services Act (PSA). It started in 2020 and gives the Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS) power to control crypto service providers. If a company lets people buy, sell, or store crypto, it must apply for a MAS license.

What Are Licensed Crypto Platforms?

Licensed exchanges follow strict rules to protect users. These rules include:

  • Know Your Customer (KYC): Verifying identity documents before letting people trade.
  • Anti-Money Laundering (AML): Checking that crypto is not used for crime.
  • Data protection: Keeping your personal information safe.

Examples of licensed crypto services in Singapore include:

  • Coinhako
  • Crypto.com
  • DBS Digital Exchange
  • OKX (with local SGD payment support)

Using these platforms is the safest way to transfer crypto to a bank account in SG. If you use an unlicensed provider, your funds might be frozen, or banks may block your transaction.

MAS also requires crypto firms to report large transfers and suspicious activities. That’s why compliance matters. Before cashing out your crypto, make sure you are using a licensed and regulated platform.

How Crypto-to-Bank Transfers Work in Singapore

Sending crypto to your bank account is possible and legal in Singapore, but it must be done the right way. You can’t just move coins directly from your wallet to a bank. You need a crypto platform that supports SGD withdrawals.

Travel Rule and How It Affects Your Transfers

When you move crypto to a bank in Singapore, there’s an important rule to know: the Travel Rule. It requires crypto platforms to share certain information about you and the person receiving the money.

The Travel Rule was created to prevent money laundering and illegal activities. Singapore follows this rule strictly through MAS Notice PSN02, which all licensed platforms must obey.

What Information Is Shared?

For every transfer, platforms must pass along details like:

  • Your full name
  • Your bank account number
  • The recipient’s name and account details
  • Transaction amount and date

This applies to every transaction, but for transfers above SGD 1,500, even more details may be required. This helps authorities track large money flows and spot suspicious actions.

How Does This Affect You?

If you use a licensed platform, you will have to complete KYC checks. Your identity and bank details will be verified. When you withdraw SGD to your bank, the platform shares your info with the bank and regulators.

If you try to send crypto from a non-custodial wallet (like MetaMask or Ledger) directly to another wallet or bank, it gets tricky. The Travel Rule does not fully cover such transfers, but platforms may ask for proof that you own the sending wallet.

Practical Tips

  • Use only MAS-licensed crypto platforms for bank transfers.
  • Prepare to submit identification documents.
  • Expect platforms to share your data with banks as part of compliance.
  • Avoid trying to bypass the Travel Rule, as it may cause delays or blocked transfers.

Following the Travel Rule helps keep your transactions safe and legal in Singapore. It also builds trust between crypto users and banks.

Role of Licensed Crypto Platforms (DPT/DTSP)

In Singapore, only crypto platforms licensed under the Payment Services Act can legally offer crypto services. These platforms are called Digital Payment Token (DPT) providers or Digital Token Service Providers (DTSP). Their role is crucial for safe and smooth crypto-to-bank transfers.

Licensed platforms follow strict rules set by the Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS). This means they:

  • Verify users’ identities to prevent fraud.
  • Monitor transactions to detect suspicious activity.
  • Keep user data secure and private.
  • Report large or unusual transactions to regulators.

By using licensed platforms, you ensure your crypto cashout in Singapore is legal and reliable. They work directly with banks to transfer funds in SGD via FAST or PayNow.

Risks of Using Unlicensed Platforms

If you use an unlicensed platform, you may face problems such as:

  • Frozen funds or blocked withdrawals.
  • Delayed or failed bank transfers.
  • Increased risk of scams or fraud.
  • Possible legal trouble for non-compliance with MAS rules.

For example, if you try to cash out crypto using an overseas exchange without a MAS license, your bank in Singapore may reject the incoming funds. This makes licensed providers your safest option.

Popular Licensed Platforms in Singapore

  • Crypto.com

How Banks in Singapore Handle Crypto Transfers

Banks in Singapore are cautious but open to working with crypto users. They don’t consider cryptocurrencies as money, but they do accept funds from licensed crypto platforms that convert digital assets to SGD.

Before accepting transfers, banks perform checks to prevent money laundering and fraud. This means they review the source of funds and confirm that the sending platform follows MAS rules.

What Banks Look For:

  • Proof that the crypto platform is licensed by MAS.
  • Clear records of your transactions and identity verification.
  • Compliance with Anti-Money Laundering (AML) and Countering the Financing of Terrorism (CFT) policies.
  • Consistency in amounts and frequency of transfers.

If your crypto exchange and bank follow these rules, your SGD deposits usually clear smoothly. Common banks in Singapore supporting these transfers include DBS, UOB, OCBC, and Citibank.

However, if you try to deposit funds from unregulated sources or unknown crypto platforms, your bank may block or freeze the transfer. That’s why working with licensed providers is important.

Working with Self-Hosted Wallets and Risks

Many Singaporeans use self-hosted wallets like Ledger, MetaMask, or Trust Wallet. These wallets give you full control of your crypto, but moving funds from them to a bank can be more complicated.

Unlike transfers through licensed exchanges, sending crypto directly from your own wallet to a bank is not straightforward. Banks and regulators require clear proof of where the funds come from.

Key Challenges

  • Travel Rule: Platforms must share sender and receiver information. Self-hosted wallets don’t have a company behind them to provide this info easily.
  • Proof of Ownership: You may need to verify that you own the wallet sending the funds. This can include signing messages or other identity checks.
  • Risk of Delays or Rejections: Banks may delay or reject incoming funds without clear transaction history.

If you want to cash out from a self-hosted wallet, the safest way is to first send crypto to a licensed exchange. There, you can sell it for SGD and withdraw to your bank smoothly.

Trying to bypass regulated platforms can lead to problems like frozen accounts or even legal trouble. Always follow local rules to keep your transactions safe.

Changes and New Rules Starting 2026

Singapore is updating its crypto regulations to make the market safer and more transparent. Starting from June 30, 2025, new rules will affect how crypto service providers operate, especially regarding foreign clients.

The Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS) will require Digital Token Service Providers (DTSPs) to stop offering services only to overseas users unless they have special licenses. This is to reduce risks related to money laundering and fraud.

What This Means for You

  • Many crypto firms will no longer serve overseas customers from Singapore without extra approval.
  • If you use an overseas platform without a MAS license, it might be harder to cash out to a Singapore bank.
  • Licensed platforms will need to improve their systems for security, risk management, and compliance.

For Singapore residents, this means more protection but also stricter checks. It’s important to use platforms that meet the new standards to avoid delays or blocked transfers.

How to Prepare

  • Check if your crypto provider has a valid MAS license.
  • Keep your KYC documents up to date.
  • Be ready for enhanced verification steps when cashing out crypto in SG.
  • Avoid using unregulated or overseas platforms without proper licensing.

These changes aim to make crypto safer and easier to use with banks in Singapore. Staying informed will help you manage your crypto-to-bank transfers smoothly in the future.

Practical Guide: How to Safely Transfer Crypto to Your Bank Account in Singapore

If you want to transfer crypto to bank SG safely and legally, follow these simple steps. This guide will help you avoid delays, extra fees, or blocked transactions.

Step 1: Choose a Licensed Crypto Platform

Always use a platform licensed by the Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS). Examples include Coinhako, Crypto.com, and OKX. These providers follow Singapore laws and work smoothly with banks.

Step 2: Complete KYC Verification

Before trading or withdrawing, you’ll need to verify your identity. This usually means uploading your ID, proof of address, and sometimes a selfie. This step is required by MAS rules to prevent fraud.

Step 3: Sell Your Crypto for SGD

Once verified, deposit your crypto to the platform and sell it for Singapore dollars (SGD). Check the current price and any fees before confirming the sale.

Step 4: Link Your Bank Account

Add your local bank account details. Make sure the account is under your name to avoid problems. Most platforms support major Singapore banks like DBS, UOB, and OCBC.

Step 5: Withdraw SGD to Your Bank

Request a withdrawal via FAST or PayNow. These payment methods are fast and secure. Transfers usually arrive within minutes or a few hours.

Additional Tips

  • Keep records of your transactions and withdrawal receipts.
  • Don’t try to send crypto directly from non-custodial wallets to banks.
