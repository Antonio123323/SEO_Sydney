Legal Status of Cryptocurrency in Singapore

As of 2026, cryptocurrency is fully legal in Singapore. You can buy, sell, hold, and use digital tokens — such as Bitcoin, Ethereum, and stablecoins — as long as you follow local regulations. However, crypto is not recognized as official currency by the Singapore government. It cannot be used to pay taxes or government fees, but it is accepted for many private transactions.

The official term for crypto in Singapore is Digital Payment Token (DPT). This category is defined under the Payment Services Act (PS Act), which regulates how digital tokens are used and traded. According to MAS, any platform offering DPT services must apply for a license and follow strict rules.

So, while crypto is allowed in Singapore, its use is governed by a clear legal framework. This makes Singapore one of the few countries where crypto usage is both legal and tightly regulated.

What Legal Crypto Means for Users

  • You can trade crypto on MAS-approved platforms that comply with regulatory standards.
  • You can hold crypto in your wallet as a long-term investment or savings strategy.
  • You can use crypto to pay for services or products through licensed payment providers.

For example, a Singaporean investor may legally buy Bitcoin via a licensed exchange, store it in a personal wallet, and later use it to purchase a laptop from a crypto-friendly retailer. All of this is legal — if done through compliant services.

Singapore’s crypto framework allows flexibility but keeps the ecosystem secure. That balance is what makes legal crypto Singapore a trusted and competitive space in Southeast Asia.

Regulatory Authorities and Framework

In Singapore, all crypto-related activities are regulated by the Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS). MAS is the country's central bank and its main financial regulator. It plays a key role in shaping crypto regulation sg and maintaining trust in the digital asset space.

Any company or individual offering crypto services — like trading, payment processing, or wallet custody — must operate under MAS oversight. The two main laws in effect are the Payment Services Act (PS Act) and the Financial Services and Markets Act (FSMA).

Main Responsibilities of MAS in the Crypto Sector

Regulatory Area MAS Role
Licensing Issues approvals for crypto exchanges, wallet providers, and DPT services.
Compliance Monitors operations to ensure companies follow KYC, AML, and CFT rules.
Risk Oversight Reviews internal controls, investor protection policies, and financial resilience.
Enforcement Has authority to fine, suspend, or shut down non-compliant crypto businesses.

To operate legally, crypto service providers in Singapore must:

  • Register with MAS under the correct licensing category (e.g., DPT provider)
  • Implement strong customer verification processes (KYC)
  • Maintain clear anti-money laundering (AML) policies

Failing to comply can lead to serious penalties — including financial fines and criminal charges. In some cases, MAS has blocked firms from operating until they meet the required standards.

2026 Updates: FSMA and DTSP Licensing

In 2026, Singapore introduced major changes to its crypto rules under the Financial Services and Markets Act (FSMA). This update was designed to close loopholes and strengthen oversight over companies offering crypto services — even if their clients are based outside the country.

The new rule introduces a special registration called the Digital Token Service Provider (DTSP) license. This is now required for any Singapore-based company that:

  • Operates a digital asset exchange platform
  • Holds or manages customer wallets
  • Transfers crypto on behalf of users

As of 30 June 2026, companies that offer these services must either obtain a DTSP license or stop serving customers — including overseas clients. This applies even if the business only operates outside Singapore but is incorporated locally.

Why FSMA Matters for the Crypto Industry

Previously, some firms based in Singapore used their local status to market themselves as “trusted” — while operating under weak compliance in other jurisdictions. FSMA now holds them to Singapore’s regulatory standards, regardless of who their clients are.

The DTSP license ensures that crypto firms meet the same strict requirements as banks and payment service providers. This includes:

  • Robust AML/CFT controls
  • Full disclosure to clients about risks
  • Independent audits and risk assessments

MAS has stated that the DTSP license will only be granted to companies with strong financial systems, experienced leadership, and a transparent business model. Many companies that couldn’t meet these conditions have either shut down or moved to other markets.

For users in Singapore, this means better protection. For businesses, it means higher responsibility and full transparency under crypto regulation sg.

What You Can and Can’t Do With Crypto in Singapore

Using crypto in Singapore is legal, but it must follow clear rules. Knowing what’s allowed — and what isn’t — can help you avoid mistakes and stay compliant with Singapore’s crypto framework.

✔️ What You Can Legally Do

  • Buy and hold digital assets for personal investment. Individuals can use licensed platforms to trade crypto in SGD or foreign currency.
  • Use crypto to pay for goods and services — if the merchant accepts it directly or through a licensed crypto payment gateway.
  • Transfer tokens between personal wallets or send them to friends and family, provided it’s not part of a business activity.
  • Trade on licensed exchanges registered with MAS (like Crypto.com or Coinbase Singapore).

⛔ What’s Not Allowed

  • Operating a crypto exchange or wallet service without a MAS license.
  • Serving foreign customers without a DTSP license (from 30 June 2026 onward).
  • Offering crypto credit products or leveraged trading to retail users.
  • Promoting crypto with misleading incentives like bonuses or giveaways targeting the general public.

Singapore also applies rules to DeFi services. If a decentralized platform is controlled by a central entity or offers services to retail users, MAS may treat it as a regulated business.

Example Scenarios

Legal: A Singapore resident buys Ethereum on a MAS-approved exchange, stores it in a private wallet, and later uses it to pay a tech freelancer in Thailand through a licensed payment provider.

Not legal: A startup in Singapore launches a crypto exchange for foreign users without applying for a DTSP license — even if it doesn't offer services locally.

AML/CFT and the Travel Rule

Singapore’s crypto laws focus heavily on transparency and security. To fight financial crime, the Monetary Authority of Singapore enforces strict rules on Anti-Money Laundering (AML) and Countering the Financing of Terrorism (CFT).

Any business dealing with digital tokens — from exchanges to wallet services — must build systems to detect, report, and prevent illegal activity. This includes:

  • Verifying user identity (KYC)
  • Monitoring large or unusual transactions
  • Reporting suspicious behavior to MAS

The Travel Rule: What It Means for Crypto Users

Since 2026, Singapore has fully enforced the Travel Rule. This global standard, recommended by the Financial Action Task Force (FATF), applies to digital transfers worth SGD 1,500 or more.

Under the Travel Rule, licensed crypto platforms must collect and transmit data about both parties involved in a qualifying transaction. This helps prevent anonymous fund movement across borders.

Information Shared Under the Travel Rule

  • Sender and recipient full names
  • Wallet addresses
  • Transaction reference or purpose (when required)
  • ID or tax information for higher-risk transactions

This rule applies even when one party uses a non-custodial wallet like MetaMask. In such cases, the platform involved in initiating the transfer is responsible for collecting the necessary data.

What does this mean for regular users? Simply put, crypto is not fully anonymous anymore when sent via regulated services. But these measures are designed to keep the ecosystem safe — and help Singapore maintain its reputation as a trusted crypto hub.

Tax Implications of Using Crypto in Singapore

Singapore is known for its attractive tax policies, and crypto is no exception. Still, knowing when taxes apply is essential to staying compliant under crypto regulation sg.

No Capital Gains Tax for Individuals

If you’re a private investor who buys and holds crypto like Bitcoin or Ethereum, you do not pay capital gains tax when you sell your tokens at a profit. This is one reason why Singapore is considered a crypto-friendly country for long-term holders.

Example: You buy 1 BTC at SGD 40,000 and sell it later at SGD 60,000. No tax is owed on the SGD 20,000 gain — if it was a personal investment, not business income.

Income Tax for Crypto Businesses

Companies and freelancers who receive crypto as payment or trade crypto as part of their main activity must pay income tax on profits.

  • A graphic designer who gets paid in crypto for services must declare it as income.
  • A crypto trading company is taxed on gains, just like any other business income.

Goods and Services Tax (GST)

Buying or selling crypto is not subject to GST. But if a platform or business charges service fees — for example, to manage wallets or convert crypto to fiat — those fees may include GST (currently 8%).

Keep Records to Stay Safe

MAS recommends that users and businesses track and document all crypto transactions, including:

  • Wallet addresses used
  • Transaction amounts and timestamps
  • Receipts or invoices (if crypto was used for payment)

Staying organized not only helps with taxes, but also protects you in case of audits or disputes.

Business and Compliance Considerations

If you're running or planning a crypto-related business in Singapore, understanding the legal framework is critical. In 2026, the bar for compliance is higher than ever — but the opportunities are also growing for those who meet MAS standards.

Who Needs a License?

Under the Payment Services Act and the Financial Services and Markets Act, you need a license if your company:

  • Operates a crypto exchange platform
  • Provides token custody or wallet services
  • Facilitates crypto payments or remittances
  • Offers fundraising or launchpad services using digital assets

From 30 June 2026, the DTSP license is also required for companies in Singapore that serve clients outside the country. Even if your business only targets foreign users, you're still subject to local law.

Types of Licenses for Crypto Businesses

  • Standard Payment Institution (SPI): For small-scale operations under SGD 3 million/month.
  • Major Payment Institution (MPI): For larger service providers with higher volumes.
  • Digital Token Service Provider (DTSP): Mandatory under FSMA for global-facing crypto firms.

Compliance Requirements

Every licensed business must:

  • Conduct KYC and AML/CFT checks
  • Monitor suspicious activity and report to MAS
  • Protect customer data and funds
  • Implement risk control systems and perform audits

Penalties for Non-Compliance

Failing to follow MAS rules can lead to:

  • Fines up to SGD 250,000
  • License suspension or cancellation
  • Criminal charges with possible imprisonment

To succeed in Singapore’s regulated crypto space, businesses must treat compliance as a core part of their strategy — not just paperwork.
