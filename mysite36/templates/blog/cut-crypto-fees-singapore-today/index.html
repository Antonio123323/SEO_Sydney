Understanding Crypto Transaction Fees

Before you start saving on fees, it's important to understand where they come from. Crypto transactions involve more than just sending tokens from one wallet to another. Each step may include hidden costs that affect how much you actually receive or send.

1. Network Fees (Blockchain Fees)

Every crypto transaction must be confirmed by a blockchain. That means paying a small fee to the network — also called a gas fee or miner fee. These fees depend on the blockchain you use.

  • Bitcoin: fees can range from S$2 to over S$10 during busy periods
  • Ethereum: gas fees change often and may be as high as S$20 for a single transfer
  • Polygon / Arbitrum / Solana: much cheaper, sometimes under S$0.01 per transaction

Tip: using low-fee blockchains like Polygon or Layer 2 networks can reduce your costs significantly.

2. Exchange Fees

When you send crypto through a central exchange like Binance or Crypto.com, you may face:

  • Trading fees: these are taken when you buy or sell crypto (usually 0.1%–0.75%)
  • Withdrawal fees: charged when sending crypto out of the platform
  • Spread fees: the hidden cost between buy and sell prices

3. Fiat Conversion Fees (SGD)

To buy or withdraw crypto using Singapore Dollars (SGD), many platforms add gateway or bank fees. These vary depending on the method used — PayNow, FAST transfer, or credit card.

Example: Some platforms charge S$1–S$5 for FAST withdrawals, while others hide the cost in conversion rates.

Understanding these three types of fees helps you make better choices. In the next section, we’ll look at how to choose the best exchange in Singapore to keep your crypto transfers low cost.

Picking the right exchange is one of the easiest ways to reduce crypto fees. Not all platforms charge the same. Some offer better rates for trading, sending, or withdrawing crypto. In Singapore, you have access to both local and global exchanges — each with different fee structures.

Choose platforms that support PayNow, FAST, or Xfers for local bank transfers. These methods are fast and often free. Avoid using credit cards or international wires, which usually include extra costs.

Strategies to Reduce Network (Blockchain) Fees

When you send crypto, a big part of the fee comes from the blockchain itself. These network fees go to miners or validators to confirm your transaction. The good news is, you can control them by choosing the right network, time, and method.

1. Use Cheaper Blockchains

Not all blockchains charge the same. Some are much cheaper than others. If you're sending tokens like USDT, choose a low-fee chain:

  • Ethereum: expensive, often S$10+ per transfer
  • Polygon: low fees, often less than S$0.01
  • Arbitrum / Optimism: Layer 2 networks for Ethereum with much lower gas costs
  • Solana: fast and cheap, usually under S$0.001 per transaction

Example: Sending USDT via Ethereum may cost S$15, but the same amount on Polygon could cost less than S$0.01.

2. Send During Off-Peak Times

Network fees change throughout the day. When the blockchain is busy, fees go up. To save money, send crypto during less active hours — usually late at night or weekends (SGT time).

Tip: Use tools like Etherscan Gas Tracker to check live Ethereum gas prices.

3. Use Layer 2 Solutions

Layer 2 networks run on top of main blockchains and process transactions faster and cheaper. They are safe and widely supported by modern wallets and exchanges.

Examples of Layer 2 networks:

  • Arbitrum – low fees, works with major DeFi apps
  • Optimism – cheaper Ethereum alternative
  • Lightning Network – fast BTC payments with tiny fees

4. Batch Your Transactions

If you need to send crypto to multiple people or wallets, try batching them into one transaction. Some wallets and exchanges support this. It reduces the total fee you pay.

Reducing network fees is all about smart planning. In the next section, we’ll explore how to save when moving funds between crypto and Singapore Dollars (SGD).

Reduce Fiat On/Off Ramp Costs in Singapore

Buying and cashing out crypto with Singapore Dollars (SGD) can also involve hidden fees. These are called fiat on-ramp and off-ramp costs. Choosing the right payment methods and platforms can save you a lot of money.

1. Use Local Payment Methods

In Singapore, the best way to move money in and out of crypto is through local bank transfers. Look for exchanges that support:

  • PayNow – instant and often free
  • FAST transfer – bank-to-bank, same day
  • Xfers – trusted local payment gateway

Tip: Avoid using credit or debit cards, which usually come with 2%–4% processing fees.

2. Check SGD Withdrawal Fees

Some platforms offer free SGD withdrawals, while others charge fixed fees or build the cost into poor exchange rates. Always read the fee section before cashing out.

Example: Independent Reserve offers free FAST withdrawals for SGD over S$1,000. Smaller amounts may have a small fee.

3. Watch Out for Conversion Spreads

Even if there’s “no fee” on paper, you may still lose money on the exchange rate. This is called a spread — the difference between buy and sell prices.

Tip: Compare SGD/USD or SGD/USDT rates across platforms before making a large conversion. A 1% spread on S$1,000 means you lose S$10.

4. Use SGD-Friendly Platforms

Some global exchanges like Coinbase don’t support SGD directly. This means double conversion and more fees. Stick with platforms like Crypto.com, Binance Singapore, Coinhako, or Independent Reserve that work with SGD directly.

Use Native Tokens for Exchange Discounts

Many crypto exchanges offer their own tokens. These are known as native tokens, and they can help you lower trading and transfer fees. If you trade often, this is an easy way to save money.

1. What Are Native Tokens?

Native tokens are issued by the platform itself. When you use them to pay fees, you usually get a discount. Some exchanges also give you other perks like faster withdrawals or access to premium features.

2. Popular Tokens to Consider

  • CRO – used on Crypto.com. Get up to 50% fee discounts when staked.
  • BNB – Binance’s token. Save 25% on trading fees when you pay with BNB.
  • KCS – KuCoin’s token. Offers trading fee rebates and passive rewards.

Example: If you make a S$1,000 trade on Binance, paying with BNB instead of USDT could save you S$2.50 immediately.

3. Things to Watch Out For

These tokens are not stable. Their value can go up or down quickly. Some exchanges require you to lock them up (stake) to get full benefits. Make sure you're comfortable with the risks before holding large amounts.

Using native tokens is optional, but if you trade regularly, it’s a smart way to reduce costs. In the next section, we’ll look at how you can avoid unnecessary swaps and transfers to prevent hidden fees.

Avoid Unnecessary Swaps and Transfers

Every time you move or swap crypto, you may pay a fee — even if it's not obvious. By keeping your actions simple and direct, you can avoid extra costs that build up over time.

1. Limit Token Swaps

It’s tempting to switch between coins — for example, from USDT to ETH, then to BTC. But each swap can come with:

  • Trading fees (0.1% to 1%)
  • Price spreads
  • Gas or network fees (especially on Ethereum)

Tip: Plan your destination token in advance. Buy only what you need. Avoid frequent conversions.

2. Reduce Wallet-to-Wallet Transfers

Sending crypto between wallets — like from an exchange to MetaMask, then to another person — adds extra steps. Each move can cost gas fees, withdrawal fees, or both.

Example: Sending ETH from Binance to MetaMask, and then to a friend, could cost over S$20 in total fees. Sending it directly from Binance to the friend’s wallet could cost only S$5.

3. Batch Transfers if Possible

If you need to send crypto to more than one person or wallet, check if your exchange supports batching. This combines multiple payments into one transaction — saving network fees.

4. Think Before You Click

Each crypto action — swap, transfer, or withdrawal — costs money. Ask yourself: is this step really needed? If not, skip it and save.

Choose the Right Wallet Type

Your crypto wallet plays a bigger role in fees than you might think. Depending on the type of wallet you use, you may pay more (or less) when sending, receiving, or managing your coins.

1. Custodial vs. Non-Custodial Wallets

  • Custodial wallets are wallets managed by an exchange or app. You don’t control the private keys.
  • Non-custodial wallets like MetaMask or Trust Wallet give you full control of your crypto — and your fees.

Tip: Custodial wallets often charge higher withdrawal fees, while non-custodial wallets give you more control over gas settings.

2. Hardware Wallets

Devices like Ledger or Trezor offer the best security but require manual steps for sending crypto. They use non-custodial settings and give full fee transparency. Great for long-term holders who want low-cost, high-control options.

3. Integrated Layer 2 Support

Modern wallets now support Layer 2 networks like Arbitrum and Optimism. Choosing a wallet with built-in Layer 2 support lets you send crypto with lower fees — often under S$0.10 per transfer.

Picking the right wallet can reduce fees and improve your crypto experience. In the next section, we’ll show a real-world example of how fees vary across platforms and networks.
