2. Regulation and Legal Status

When it comes to sending money in Singapore, regulations play a big role. The Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS) is the main body that oversees both traditional and crypto transfers. It works to protect users and prevent illegal activity like fraud and money laundering.

Traditional Transfers

Banks and licensed money transfer companies in Singapore operate under the Payment Services Act (PSA). They must follow strict rules, including identity checks (KYC) and reporting large transactions. These systems are stable and well-trusted, but they can be slow and expensive.

Crypto Transfers

Crypto companies are also regulated under the same PSA. But they need a special license to provide Digital Payment Token (DPT) services. Popular platforms like Crypto.com, Coinbase, and OKX have already received licenses or in-principle approval from MAS.

In 2024, MAS introduced new rules for stablecoins—crypto tokens linked to fiat currencies like USD or SGD. Licensed stablecoins must meet strict standards for backing, redemption, and transparency. This makes them more suitable for payments and remittances.

What This Means for You

  • If you use a licensed crypto platform in Singapore, your transfers are protected by local laws.
  • If you use an unlicensed platform, you may face risks like scams or blocked transactions.

MAS is also working with banks and fintechs to create a safe space where traditional and crypto systems can work together. The result is a growing number of hybrid services that let users move funds between crypto and SGD easily and legally.

Understanding the legal side helps you avoid problems and choose reliable platforms like Immediate FastX that operate under clear rules.

3. Speed and Accessibility

One of the biggest reasons people switch to crypto is speed. In Singapore, time matters—especially for those sending money to family overseas or paying for urgent services.

How Fast Are Traditional Bank Transfers?

Bank transfers in Singapore are usually reliable, but not always fast. Local transfers using FAST or PayNow are instant. But cross-border transfers can take 1 to 3 business days, especially when using SWIFT or going through multiple banks.

For example, sending money from Singapore to the Philippines or India using a traditional bank might take more than 48 hours, depending on time zones, weekends, and currency conversions.

How Fast Is Crypto?

Crypto transfers can happen in minutes. If you use networks like Tron (TRC-20) or Stellar, sending stablecoins like USDT or USDC is usually completed in under 60 seconds. There’s no need to wait for bank hours or clearances.

Who Can Access These Services?

Accessibility is also key. With banks, you need an account, ID, and often proof of income. Some people, like migrant workers or freelancers, may find this difficult.

Crypto, on the other hand, is open to anyone with a smartphone and internet. No bank account? No problem. Just create a wallet and you can send crypto in SG to anywhere in the world.

4. Cost and Fees

Sending money isn't free. Whether you use a bank or crypto, there are always costs involved. But the size and type of those costs vary a lot.

Traditional Bank Transfers

Banks in Singapore charge a variety of fees. For local transfers, it's often free or very low cost. But international transfers can be expensive. You might pay:

  • SGD 10 to SGD 30 per transfer

These fees add up, especially if you send money every month. For small payments, the cost can be more than 5% of the total amount.

Crypto costs are usually lower—but they depend on the blockchain used. Some of the cheapest options in Singapore include:

6. Risks and Security

Every money transfer method has risks. Whether you use a bank or send crypto, safety should always come first. Singapore has strong laws to protect users, but knowing the risks helps you avoid problems.

Risks of Bank Transfers

Traditional bank transfers are generally safe, but not risk-free. Common problems include:

  • Technical errors: Server outages or maintenance can delay your transfer.
  • Wrong information: A small mistake in the account number can send your money to the wrong person. Getting it back may take days—or never happen.
  • Delays: Large amounts or overseas transfers often trigger manual checks, slowing things down.

Risks of Crypto Transfers

Crypto is fast and efficient, but it carries its own dangers. These are the most common:

  • Phishing scams: Fake websites trick users into entering private keys or passwords.
  • Fake tokens or platforms: Some projects promise big returns but disappear with your money.
  • No recovery: If you send crypto to the wrong address, there’s no way to reverse it.

Scams in Singapore

Crypto scams are on the rise. In one high-profile case, Singaporean actor Lawrence Pang lost SGD 40,000 after falling for a fake investment scheme. In total, people in Singapore lost over SGD 1 billion to online scams in recent years—many involving cryptocurrency.

How Singapore Is Responding

The Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS) has introduced new rules to protect users:

  • Licensing for all crypto service providers
  • Strict KYC (Know Your Customer) and AML (Anti-Money Laundering) checks
  • Delayed withdrawals to block suspicious transactions

How to Stay Safe

Whether you use a bank or crypto, follow these tips:

  • Always double-check recipient details
  • Use only licensed and trusted platforms like Immediate FastX
  • Enable 2FA and don’t share passwords or seed phrases

In the end, security is about both smart technology and smart users. Be informed, be alert, and choose services with strong protection policies.
