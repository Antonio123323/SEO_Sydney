2. Regulation and Legal Status

In Singapore, all money-related services are tightly regulated. This includes both banks and crypto platforms. The goal is to keep transfers safe, prevent fraud, and protect users from scams or loss.

How Banks Are Regulated

Traditional money transfers are handled by licensed banks and financial companies. They follow the rules set by the Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS) under the Payment Services Act (PSA). These rules require banks to:

  • Verify customer identity (KYC)
  • Track transactions for illegal activity (AML)
  • Report suspicious behavior

This system offers trust and legal clarity—but it can also create delays and higher costs for users.

What About Crypto in Singapore?

Crypto platforms are also covered by the PSA. To operate legally, they must apply for a special license to provide Digital Payment Token (DPT) services. Companies like OKX, Crypto.com, and Coinbase have already received approvals to serve users in Singapore.

In 2024, MAS introduced tighter rules for stablecoins. These are digital currencies tied to regular money (like SGD or USD). To be approved, stablecoins must meet strict standards for value backing, redemption, and transparency.

Why This Matters

Whether you’re using a bank or crypto, regulation keeps the system safe. But only licensed platforms offer full protection. That’s why it’s important to use trusted services like Immediate FastX, which comply with MAS guidelines and offer secure, legal transfers.

The legal system in Singapore supports innovation—but not at the cost of security. That balance helps both crypto and banks operate side by side, giving users more choice and control.

3. Speed and Accessibility

When sending money, time matters. Whether you're paying bills, helping family, or managing a business transaction—speed can make a big difference. In this area, crypto and traditional bank transfers offer very different experiences.

Transfer Speeds: Banks vs Crypto

Bank transfers within Singapore, such as through FAST or PayNow, are usually instant. But once you go international, the situation changes. Overseas transfers can take anywhere from 1 to 3 business days. That delay often comes from time zone differences, banking hours, and manual checks.

Crypto transfers, by contrast, can be almost instant. Popular blockchain networks like Tron (TRC-20) or Stellar can process payments in seconds. There's no need to wait for a working day, no middlemen, and no manual approvals.

Who Can Use These Services?

Access is another key factor. Traditional banking services require a valid ID, proof of address, and in some cases, employment documents. This can be difficult for new migrants, freelancers, or those with no fixed income.

Crypto systems are far more open. Anyone with a phone, internet, and a crypto wallet can start sending money. There are no barriers like income checks or local bank accounts.

Example: Sending SGD 500

If you're sending SGD 500 to a friend in Malaysia, a bank transfer might cost SGD 10 and take two days. Using crypto, the same amount could be sent in minutes with fees as low as SGD 0.50—depending on the blockchain used.

For people who need fast, flexible transfers, crypto clearly stands out. But it works best when users understand the system and choose trusted tools.

4. Cost and Fees

Cost is one of the most important factors when choosing how to send money. Traditional services and crypto transfers both come with fees—but they are structured very differently.

Fees with Bank Transfers

When you use a bank to send money overseas, you usually pay multiple charges. These may include:

  • Transfer fee: Often between SGD 10 and SGD 30
  • Currency exchange markup: Rates are usually worse than market average
  • Intermediary bank fees: In cross-border transfers, other banks in the network may take a cut

Even if the initial fee seems low, the hidden costs can be high. In some cases, the total expense reaches 5–7% of the transfer amount.

Fees with Crypto Transfers

Crypto offers a different model. Most networks charge a small transaction fee, called a “gas fee.” This fee varies depending on the network you use:

  • TRC-20 (Tron): Less than SGD 1 per transfer
  • Stellar: Often less than SGD 0.01
  • Ethereum: Can be higher, especially when the network is busy

There are also no currency conversion losses if you’re sending and receiving stablecoins like USDT or USDC. What you send is what the receiver gets.

6. Risks and Security

No money transfer system is perfect. Whether you use a bank or send crypto, there are always risks. In Singapore, strict rules reduce many of these problems—but users still need to be cautious.

What Can Go Wrong with Bank Transfers?

Banks are trusted for a reason, but issues still happen. The most common include:

  • System outages: Scheduled maintenance or technical errors can block transactions for hours.
  • Wrong account details: A single typo may send money to the wrong recipient, and recovery is often slow.
  • Processing delays: Large amounts or unusual activity may trigger reviews that delay transfers.

Even though banks follow strict rules, users have little control once a transfer is sent.

Risks of Using Crypto

Crypto allows fast transfers, but mistakes are usually permanent. Risks include:

  • Fake websites and apps: Scammers create lookalike platforms to steal user info and funds.
  • Fraudulent tokens: Some “investment projects” are actually scams designed to disappear with your money.
  • Unrecoverable errors: Sending to the wrong crypto address is irreversible—there is no refund button.

Case from Singapore

In a widely reported case, a local actor lost SGD 40,000 in a crypto scam promising high profits. He believed the platform was legitimate, but it was part of a fake investment scheme. This case reflects a larger trend: Singaporeans have lost over SGD 1 billion to online scams in recent years, with crypto playing a major role.

How Authorities Respond

The Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS) has introduced new rules to protect users:

  • Licensing for crypto service providers
  • Mandatory identity verification (KYC)
  • Delayed fund withdrawals to stop fast-moving fraud

Many platforms now also add extra verification steps before crypto is transferred—especially for first-time users or large amounts.

Staying Safe

To avoid problems, always:

  • Use only regulated services
  • Double-check all payment details before confirming
  • Protect your devices and wallet access credentials

Security isn’t just about technology—it’s also about habits. Whether you choose crypto or bank transfers, staying alert is the best defense.
