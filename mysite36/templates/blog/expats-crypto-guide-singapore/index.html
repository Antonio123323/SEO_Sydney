Legal Regulations of Crypto in Singapore

Singapore is known for its strong financial rules and clear crypto policy. The central authority that regulates digital assets is the Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS). If you’re an expat using crypto here, it’s important to understand how the law works.

Licensing and Regulation

Crypto companies in Singapore must register under the Payment Services Act (PSA) and, in many cases, the Financial Services and Markets Act (FSMA). These laws control how companies handle digital payments and protect users from fraud or money laundering.

To operate legally, platforms must get a Digital Payment Token (DPT) license. This license ensures the platform meets safety, financial, and cybersecurity standards. As a user, choosing a licensed platform means your money and data are safer.

New Rules in 2026

From June 30, 2026, new rules will require all crypto providers based in Singapore to be licensed — even if they serve only international customers. This means that any platform not licensed by MAS must stop operations in Singapore, or face heavy penalties.

As an expat, this change is important. You should use platforms that are fully licensed in Singapore, such as OKX or Coinhako, or trusted global players with a local presence.

Why It Matters

  • Safety: Regulated platforms must follow strict security and operational standards.

Crypto Taxes for Expats

One of the biggest questions for expats using crypto in Singapore is taxes. The good news? Singapore is one of the most tax-friendly countries when it comes to cryptocurrencies.

No Capital Gains Tax

Singapore does not tax capital gains. That means if you buy Bitcoin and sell it later for a profit, you don’t pay tax on that gain — as long as you’re not running a business based on trading. This applies to most retail investors, including expats.

When Taxes Apply

There are some situations where tax may apply:

  • Trading as a business: If crypto is your main income source and you trade daily, the profits may be taxed as income.
  • Mining or staking: Income from mining or staking may be considered taxable, depending on how often and how much you earn.
  • GST (Goods and Services Tax): Some crypto transactions, especially involving local services, may be subject to GST (currently 8%).

Tax Reporting for Expats

If you’re a foreigner living in Singapore, you need to check the tax rules both in Singapore and in your home country. For example:

  • Singapore doesn’t require tax on capital gains, but your home country might.
  • Bank transfers from crypto exchanges may need to be declared if audited.

Security and Protecting Your Crypto Assets

Crypto gives you full control of your money — but that also means you are fully responsible for its safety. Expats must be extra careful to protect their crypto from theft, scams, or mistakes.

Common Security Risks

  • Phishing: Fake emails or websites that try to steal your login info.
  • Hacked exchanges: Platforms without strong security can be breached.
  • Lost passwords: Without backup access, your funds could be gone forever.

To stay safe, always take a few basic steps:

How to Protect Your Crypto

  • Enable 2FA (Two-Factor Authentication): Always activate 2FA on your account to prevent unauthorized access.
  • Use strong, unique passwords: Don’t reuse the same password across accounts.
  • Store long-term holdings in a hardware wallet: Cold wallets like Ledger or Trezor are safer for large amounts.
  • Keep backups: Write down your seed phrases and store them in a safe place, offline.
  • Be careful with links: Never click suspicious emails or download files from unknown sources.
