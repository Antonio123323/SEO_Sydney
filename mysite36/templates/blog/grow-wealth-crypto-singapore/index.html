Legal Regulations of Crypto in Singapore

Singapore has built a reputation as a safe and forward-looking hub for digital finance. If you're living here as an expat and using crypto, it's essential to understand how the rules work. The main authority that oversees digital asset activity is the Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS).

Regulatory Framework and Licensing

All crypto-related businesses in Singapore must comply with two key laws: the Payment Services Act (PSA) and the Financial Services and Markets Act (FSMA). These frameworks are designed to prevent money laundering, protect users, and bring transparency to the industry.

To legally offer crypto services, companies must hold a Digital Payment Token (DPT) license. This license confirms that the provider meets Singapore's standards for financial security and consumer protection. For users, choosing a licensed platform reduces the risk of fraud or platform failure.

Upcoming Changes in 2026

Starting from June 30, 2026, new rules will come into effect. Any crypto platform operating from Singapore — even if it only serves clients abroad — must be fully licensed by MAS. Non-compliant operators will face legal consequences and may have to shut down.

For expats, this means it's important to use platforms that are properly regulated under Singaporean law. Choosing a trusted, approved provider is no longer optional — it’s necessary for legal and financial safety.

Crypto Taxes for Expats

Taxes can be a concern for anyone dealing with digital assets — especially when living abroad. Fortunately, Singapore offers a very friendly tax environment for cryptocurrency users, including foreign residents.

No Tax on Capital Gains

One of the most attractive features of Singapore’s tax system is the zero tax on capital gains. If you buy cryptocurrency and later sell it at a profit, you usually don’t owe any tax — unless your trading activity is considered a business.

This applies to most casual investors and expats using crypto to hold long-term assets or transfer funds.

When Crypto Becomes Taxable

There are certain cases where your crypto activity may trigger tax obligations:

  • Frequent trading as a business: If crypto trading is your main job or generates regular income, it may be treated as business income and taxed accordingly.
  • Crypto mining or staking: Earnings from mining or staking can be considered taxable if they’re part of a commercial setup.
  • GST (Goods and Services Tax): While most crypto trades are exempt, some services tied to local businesses may involve an 8% GST.

Cross-Border Considerations

As an expat, your tax responsibilities might also depend on your citizenship or home country’s laws. For example, U.S. citizens may have to declare overseas crypto holdings to the IRS, even if they live in Singapore.

It’s important to keep accurate records of your activity, including transfers, trades, and wallet balances.

Security and Protecting Your Crypto Assets

One of the most important parts of working with crypto is keeping it safe. Unlike banks, crypto gives you full control — but also full responsibility. That’s why security must always come first.

Top Risks to Watch Out For

  • Phishing attacks: Fake websites or emails that steal your login details.
  • Exchange hacks: Platforms with weak systems may be breached.
  • Lost credentials: If you lose your wallet recovery phrase, your funds may be gone forever.

Smart Protection Steps

To protect your crypto, follow these basic safety practices:

  • Enable 2FA: Two-factor authentication adds a second layer of login protection.
  • Use unique passwords: Don’t reuse passwords across multiple platforms.
  • Store long-term assets in cold wallets: Devices like Ledger or Trezor offer offline safety.
  • Backup your seed phrase: Write it down and store it securely — never online.
  • Be cautious with links and messages: Avoid clicking suspicious files or unknown contacts.
